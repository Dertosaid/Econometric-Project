In this project, we will analyze various factors in terms of their impact on life expectancy. Our dependent variable is: -life expectancy, and our explanatory variables are: -access to clean fuels and technologies for cooking, -children per woman, -gdp per capita, -share of adults defined as obese, -share of adults who smoke, -share of individuals using the internet, -share of the population with access to electocity.

In this project we will consider 3 main hypotheses:

H1: Economic development (measured by GDP per capita) has a positive and diminishing marginal impact on life expectancy.

H2: Lifestyle risks, specifically smoking and obesity rates, are expected to have a significant negative impact on longevity.

H3: Infrastructure indicators, such as access to electricity and clean fuels, are vital determinants, especially in developing nations.

All the data we use comes from Our World in Data, which is a reputable source for global statistics and research. The data is collected from various sources, including international organizations, research institutions, and government agencies. It is regularly updated and covers a wide range of topics, making it a valuable resource for our analysis of life expectancy and its determinants.

Now let’s choose the optimal set of variables, for this task we will use a Hellwig method, which evaluates the explanatory power of each variable in relation to the dependent variable and the redundancy among the explanatory variables. The method calculates a “quality index” for each variable, which helps us identify which variables contribute most to explaining the variance in life expectancy while minimizing redundancy.

# Calculate the correlation matrix for the numeric variables in the training dataset, the factorial varaibles will be includes in the model as countries definitely have impact on the life expectancy.

numeric_data <- train_data %>% select(where(is.numeric), -GDP) # usuwamy surowe GDP, zostawiamy log_GDP

cor_matrix <- cor(numeric_data)

r0 <- cor_matrix["LifeExp", -which(colnames(cor_matrix) == "LifeExp")]
r <-cor_matrix[-which(rownames(cor_matrix) == "LifeExp"), -which(colnames(cor_matrix) == "LifeExp")]

scenarios <- expand.grid(rep(list(c(TRUE, FALSE)), ncol(r) )) 
H_final<-c()

for(i in 1:nrow(scenarios) ) {
  indices<-which(as.logical(scenarios[i,]))
  if(length(indices)==0) {
    H_final[i]<-0
    next
  }
  H<-c()
  for(j in 1:length(indices)) {
     H[j]<- r0[indices[j]]^2 / sum(abs(r[indices[j],indices]))
  }
  H_final[i]<-sum(H)
}


best_idx <- which.max(H_final)

best_vars <- colnames(r)[as.logical(scenarios[best_idx, ])]

print(best_vars)

The initial estimation of the full model yields a high Adjusted R-squared (0.8152), indicating that our variables collectively explain about 81.5% of the variance in global life expectancy. As expected, log_GDP and Fertility are highly significant (\(p < 0.001\)), and the Continent dummy variables confirm significant regional differences (e.g., a strong positive effect for Europe and Oceania compared to the African baseline). However, the infrastructure variables (Internet, Electricity, CleanFuels) and lifestyle factors (Smoking, Obesity) lack statistical significance. In econometric modeling, such a scenario—where the overall model fit is high but individual predictors are insignificant—strongly suggests the presence of multicollinearity. To diagnose this, we must examine the Variance Inflation Factor (VIF).

vif(model_full)

The VIF diagnostic test clearly explains the lack of significance in our previous model. The VIF values for log_GDP (8.73), CleanFuels (7.40), and Internet (7.23) severely exceed the standard acceptable threshold of 5, indicating extreme multicollinearity. Access to electricity, internet, and clean fuels is so strongly coupled with a country’s wealth that including them alongside GDP artificially inflates the standard errors of our estimators.

From a scientific standpoint, this compels us to reject Hypothesis 3 (H3): at the macroeconomic level, basic infrastructure does not act as an independent driver of longevity, but is rather a direct function of economic development (log_GDP). Consequently, to establish a robust and mathematically sound model, we will remove the redundant infrastructure variables (Internet, Electricity, CleanFuels) and estimate a reduced model. This will allow us to properly test our remaining hypotheses (H1 and H2) without the distortion of variance inflation.
